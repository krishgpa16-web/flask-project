Supervised Learning · Regression

Linear
Regression

Linear Regression models the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables by fitting a straight line — or hyperplane — through the data. It minimizes the sum of squared residuals to find the best-fit parameters, making it one of the most interpretable models in all of statistics.

Core Equation
ŷ = β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + ··· + βₙxₙ + ε

where β are coefficients learned by OLS and ε is the error term

  • 01

    Ordinary Least Squares

    Minimizes Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² — the sum of squared differences between observed and predicted values.

  • 02

    Key Assumptions

    Linearity, independence of errors, homoscedasticity, no multicollinearity, normality of residuals.

  • 03

    Regularization Variants

    Ridge (L2), Lasso (L1), and ElasticNet add penalty terms to prevent overfitting on high-dimensional data.

  • 04

    Evaluation Metrics

    R² score, Mean Squared Error (MSE), Root MSE, and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) quantify model fit.

Supervised Learning · Ensemble

Random
Forest

Random Forest is an ensemble method that builds hundreds of Decision Trees on random subsets of data and features, then aggregates their predictions via majority vote (classification) or averaging (regression). Introduced by Leo Breiman in 2001, it remains one of the most powerful out-of-the-box algorithms in ML.

Prediction Formula
ŷ = (1/B) Σᵦ hᵦ(x)

B = number of trees, hᵦ = individual tree prediction

  • 01

    Bootstrap Aggregation (Bagging)

    Each tree is trained on a random bootstrap sample of the training data, reducing variance.

  • 02

    Feature Randomness

    At each split, only a random subset of √p features are considered, decorrelating the trees.

  • 03

    Out-of-Bag (OOB) Error

    ~37% of samples are left out per tree, enabling free cross-validation without extra splits.

  • 04

    Feature Importance

    Mean Decrease in Impurity (MDI) or permutation importance ranks predictive features automatically.
